Section 1

Defining Artificial Intelligence

Let's start with the fundamentals. What do we actually mean when we say "Artificial Intelligence"? The answer is more nuanced than most people think.

What Is Artificial Intelligence?

"The science and engineering of making intelligent machines, especially intelligent computer programs."
‚Äî John McCarthy, 1956

In practice, AI is software that performs tasks typically requiring human intelligence:

  • Understanding language
  • Recognizing patterns in data
  • Making decisions under uncertainty
  • Generating new content
John McCarthy coined the term in 1956. Notice his definition says "intelligent" ‚Äî not "conscious" or "alive." AI is about capability, not sentience. Modern AI excels at narrow tasks but has no understanding of what it's doing.

The AI ‚Üí ML ‚Üí DL Hierarchy

Artificial Intelligence
Any system that mimics human intelligence
Machine Learning
Systems that learn from data
Deep Learning
Neural networks with many layers
Think of this as nested circles. All deep learning is machine learning, and all machine learning is AI ‚Äî but not the other way around. A rule-based spam filter is AI but not ML. A decision tree is ML but not deep learning. GPT-4 is all three.

This distinction matters for procurement and architecture. If your problem is deterministic and rule-based, you don't need AI ‚Äî you need automation. AI shines when the rules are too complex to write by hand, or when the problem space changes over time.

Section 2

Types of Machine Learning

Now that we've placed ML in the AI hierarchy, let's look at the three fundamental approaches to machine learning. Each solves a different class of problems.

Supervised Learning

How It Works

Model learns from labeled examples ‚Äî input/output pairs.

Like a student studying with an answer key.

  • Classification: Is this email spam or not?
  • Regression: What will this house sell for?

Enterprise Examples

  • üè¶ JPMorgan ‚Äî fraud transaction detection
  • üìß Gmail ‚Äî spam filtering (99.9% accuracy)
  • üè• PathAI ‚Äî cancer cell classification
  • üìä Salesforce Einstein ‚Äî lead scoring
Supervised learning is the workhorse of enterprise AI. You need labeled data ‚Äî which is often the hardest and most expensive part. Gmail's spam filter was trained on billions of labeled emails. The model learned patterns humans could never manually code.

Unsupervised Learning

How It Works

Model finds hidden patterns in data without labels.

Like sorting a jigsaw puzzle by color without the box image.

  • Clustering: Group similar customers
  • Anomaly Detection: Spot the outlier
  • Dimensionality Reduction: Simplify complex data

Enterprise Examples

  • üõí Amazon ‚Äî customer segmentation
  • üîê CrowdStrike ‚Äî threat anomaly detection
  • üéµ Spotify ‚Äî music clustering for Discover Weekly
  • üí≥ Visa ‚Äî unusual transaction patterns
Unsupervised learning is critical for security. You can't label every possible cyberattack ‚Äî there are always novel threats. Instead, you train a model on "normal" behavior and flag anything that deviates. CrowdStrike uses this approach to detect zero-day threats.

Reinforcement Learning

How It Works

Agent learns by trial and error ‚Äî receives rewards or penalties.

Like training a dog: treat for good behavior, no treat for bad.

  • Agent: The decision-maker
  • Environment: The world it acts in
  • Reward signal: Feedback on actions

Enterprise Examples

  • ü§ñ DeepMind AlphaGo ‚Äî defeated world champion (2016)
  • üöó Waymo ‚Äî autonomous vehicle navigation
  • üì¶ Amazon ‚Äî warehouse robot routing
  • üß† ChatGPT ‚Äî RLHF (learning from human feedback)
Reinforcement learning is how ChatGPT was refined. After pre-training on text, human raters ranked outputs, and the model was trained to maximize those rankings. This process ‚Äî RLHF ‚Äî is why ChatGPT feels more helpful than a raw language model.

Neural Networks & Deep Learning

Now let's go deeper ‚Äî literally. Neural networks are the engine behind modern AI. Understanding their basics will help you evaluate AI tools and communicate with technical teams.

Neural Networks 101

Each connection has a weight (strength)

Training = adjusting weights to minimize errors

  • Neuron: Takes inputs, applies weights, produces output
  • Layer: Group of neurons processing at the same stage
  • "Deep": Many hidden layers (GPT-4 has ~120 layers)
Neural networks are loosely inspired by the brain, but don't take the analogy too far. A neuron in a network is just a math function ‚Äî multiply inputs by weights, add a bias, apply an activation function. "Deep" learning simply means many layers stacked together.

Section 4

Large Language Models (LLMs)

LLMs are the most visible AI technology today. Let's understand what they actually do, how they work, and what they can and can't do.

What Is a Large Language Model?

An LLM is a neural network trained on massive text data to predict the next token in a sequence.

Key Properties

  • Large: Billions of parameters (GPT-4: ~1.8T estimated)
  • Language: Trained primarily on text
  • Model: Statistical model of language patterns

What They Learn

  • Grammar and syntax
  • Facts and relationships
  • Reasoning patterns
  • Code structure
The key insight: LLMs don't "know" anything. They've learned statistical patterns in language. When GPT-4 tells you the capital of France, it's not looking it up ‚Äî it's predicting that "Paris" is the most likely next token given the context. This is both their power and their limitation.

Next-Token Prediction

The model picks one token, appends it, then predicts the next one. Repeat.

This is the entire secret. An LLM generates text one token at a time, each time asking "given everything before this point, what's the most likely next piece?" It's autocomplete on steroids ‚Äî but fundamentally, it's the same principle as your phone keyboard suggestions.

The Transformer Architecture

Key Innovation: Self-Attention ‚Äî allows the model to weigh which parts of the input matter most for each output token. "Attention Is All You Need" (Vaswani et al., 2017)

The Transformer architecture replaced older sequential models (RNNs) with parallel self-attention. This means the model can look at all words simultaneously and decide which ones are most relevant. This parallelism also made training much faster on GPUs.

Notice the pattern: long periods of slow progress punctuated by breakthroughs. The field experienced two "AI winters" ‚Äî periods where funding dried up because promises outpaced reality. We may be in an AI summer now, but history teaches us to be pragmatic.

AI Winters: Hype Cycles Are Real

First AI Winter (1974‚Äì1980)

  • Perceptrons couldn't solve XOR problem
  • Government funding cut dramatically
  • Promises far exceeded capabilities

Second AI Winter (1987‚Äì1993)

  • Expert systems were brittle and expensive
  • LISP machine market collapsed
  • Japan's 5th Gen Computer project failed

Lesson for enterprises: Adopt AI for real problems with measurable ROI ‚Äî not because of hype.

Every enterprise leader should know about AI winters. The current excitement is real ‚Äî the technology works ‚Äî but not every AI project will succeed. Gartner reports that 85% of AI projects fail to deliver. The difference between success and failure is disciplined implementation, not technology choice.

Myth vs. Reality

üö´ Myth

"AI is sentient and understands what it's saying."

‚úÖ Reality

LLMs are statistical pattern matchers. They predict likely text ‚Äî they don't understand meaning, have beliefs, or experience consciousness.

üö´ Myth

"AI is always accurate and factual."

‚úÖ Reality

LLMs hallucinate confidently. A 2025 Stanford study found GPT-4 fabricated legal citations in 6% of responses. Always verify critical outputs.

These myths are dangerous in enterprise settings. If your team thinks AI output is always accurate, they'll skip verification ‚Äî and that's how you get lawyers citing fake cases (which happened to a New York attorney in 2023, who was sanctioned by the court). AI is a tool, not an oracle.

Myth vs. Reality (Continued)

üö´ Myth

"AI will replace all jobs."

‚úÖ Reality

AI automates tasks, not jobs. Goldman Sachs estimates 300M jobs will be affected, but most will be augmented, not eliminated. Radiologists now use AI to be faster, not to be replaced.

üö´ Myth

"More data always means better AI."

‚úÖ Reality

Data quality matters more than quantity. Garbage in, garbage out. IBM found that poor data quality costs US businesses $3.1 trillion per year.

The jobs myth causes unnecessary panic. The more productive framing: AI will make some skills more valuable and others less. People who can work effectively with AI will outperform those who can't. That's exactly why you're in this course.

Code: Your First AI Classification

from sklearn.datasets import load_iris
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score

# Load a classic ML dataset
data = load_iris()
X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(
    data.data, data.target, test_size=0.2, random_state=42
)

# Train a supervised learning model
model = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=100, random_state=42)
model.fit(X_train, y_train)

# Predict and evaluate
predictions = model.predict(X_test)
accuracy = accuracy_score(y_test, predictions)
print(f"Model accuracy: {accuracy:.1%}")  # ~96.7%

# This is supervised learning:
# - Labeled data (iris species)
# - Classification task
# - Learns from examples, generalizes to new data
This is a complete, runnable example. We'll run it in the lab, but the point here is to show that ML isn't magic ‚Äî it's about 10 lines of code. The complexity is in the data preparation, evaluation, and deployment ‚Äî not the model training itself. Scikit-learn makes the basics accessible to anyone who can write Python.
