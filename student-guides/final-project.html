Final Project Brief: AI Adoption Plan

Course: AI for the Enterprise: From Zero to Secure Adoption
Instructor: Howard Mukanda
Version: 1.0 — February 2026

Overview

Your final project is to create a complete AI Adoption Plan for a real or fictional organization. This plan should demonstrate that you can move from identifying an AI opportunity to designing a secure, practical, and measurable path to implementation.

You may base this on your actual workplace or create a realistic fictional company. If using your real workplace, do not include confidential data — use anonymized or generalized information.

Deliverable

One written document (PDF or Word), 2,000–4,000 words, containing all eight components listed below.

Submission: Upload through the Kajabi course portal (Module 10).
Deadline: Within 30 days of completing Module 10.

The Eight Components

1. Opportunity Identification (10 points)

Describe the business problem or opportunity you want to address with AI.

  • What department or function does this affect?
  • What is the current process (manual, semi-automated, etc.)?
  • Why is AI a good fit for this problem?
  • What is the expected business impact (time saved, cost reduced, quality improved)?

2. Tool Selection (10 points)

Recommend one or more AI tools for your use case.

  • What tools did you evaluate? (Name at least 3)
  • What criteria did you use? (Cost, security, integration, ease of use, vendor reputation)
  • Why did you choose the recommended tool(s)?
  • Is this a build, buy, or hybrid approach? Justify your choice.

3. AI Policy (15 points)

Draft an AI acceptable use policy for your organization (or the relevant department).

  • Approved tools and platforms
  • Data classification rules (what data can and cannot be used with AI)
  • Prohibited uses
  • Employee responsibilities
  • Incident reporting process
  • Review and update cadence

4. Pilot Design (15 points)

Design a pilot program to test your AI solution before full rollout.

  • Scope: Which team, process, or workflow will participate?
  • Duration: How long will the pilot run?
  • Success metrics: What specific, measurable outcomes will you track?
  • Data requirements: What data will the pilot need, and how will you handle it securely?
  • Go/no-go criteria: What results would justify moving to full rollout vs. stopping?

5. Risk Assessment (15 points)

Identify and address the key risks of your AI adoption.

  • List at least 5 risks (security, privacy, compliance, operational, reputational, ethical)
  • For each risk: likelihood (low/medium/high), impact (low/medium/high), and mitigation strategy
  • Address data privacy specifically (PII handling, data residency, vendor access)
  • Address AI-specific risks (hallucinations, bias, prompt injection, shadow AI)

6. Training Plan (10 points)

Describe how you will prepare your team for the new AI tool.

  • Who needs training? (Roles, levels)
  • What training format? (Workshop, e-learning, documentation, peer coaching)
  • What topics will training cover? (Tool usage, policy, security, when to escalate)
  • How will you measure training effectiveness?

7. Budget Estimate (10 points)

Provide a realistic cost estimate for your AI adoption.

  • Tool/platform costs (subscription, API usage, licensing)
  • Implementation costs (integration, customization, consulting)
  • Training costs (time, materials, external trainers)
  • Ongoing costs (maintenance, monitoring, support)
  • Present as a simple table with monthly or annual figures

8. KPIs and Measurement (15 points)

Define how you will measure the success of your AI adoption over time.

  • List at least 5 KPIs with clear definitions
  • Specify baseline (current state) and target for each KPI
  • Define measurement frequency (weekly, monthly, quarterly)
  • Describe your reporting approach (dashboard, monthly review, executive summary)
  • Include at least one KPI related to security/compliance and one related to user adoption

Grading Rubric

Component Points Excellent (90-100%) Good (70-89%) Needs Work (<70%)
Opportunity Identification 10 Clear problem, strong business case, specific impact Reasonable problem, some business justification Vague problem, no measurable impact
Tool Selection 10 3+ tools compared, clear criteria, justified choice 2 tools compared, basic criteria Single tool, no comparison
AI Policy 15 Comprehensive, covers all elements, ready to implement Covers most elements, some gaps Missing major elements
Pilot Design 15 Specific scope, measurable metrics, clear go/no-go General scope, some metrics defined Vague scope, no metrics
Risk Assessment 15 5+ risks with mitigations, AI-specific risks addressed 3-4 risks with basic mitigations Fewer than 3 risks, no mitigations
Training Plan 10 Role-specific, multiple formats, effectiveness measured General plan, some structure Minimal plan, no measurement
Budget Estimate 10 Detailed table, realistic figures, all cost categories Basic table, most categories covered Missing categories, unrealistic
KPIs and Measurement 15 5+ KPIs, baselines and targets, security KPI included 3-4 KPIs, some targets Fewer than 3 KPIs, no targets
Total 100

Passing score: 70 out of 100 points.

Formatting Guidelines

  • Length: 2,000–4,000 words (excluding tables and policy text)
  • Format: PDF or Word document
  • Structure: Use the eight component headings above as your sections
  • Tables: Use tables for budget, risk assessment, and KPIs
  • Citations: If you reference specific tools, vendors, or frameworks, include a brief source

Example Scenario (For Inspiration)

Company: Mid-sized insurance firm (500 employees)
Opportunity: Use AI to automate first-pass review of claims documents, reducing processing time from 48 hours to 4 hours
Tool: Enterprise-tier document AI platform (e.g., Azure AI Document Intelligence)
Key Risk: PII in claims documents — requires strict data residency and access controls
KPI: Average claims processing time, accuracy of automated extraction, employee satisfaction with new workflow

Tips for a Strong Submission

  1. Be specific. "Reduce processing time by 40%" is better than "make things faster."
  2. Be realistic. A well-reasoned plan for a modest improvement scores higher than an unrealistic moonshot.
  3. Show your reasoning. Explain why you made each decision, not just what you decided.
  4. Address security throughout. Security should appear in your policy, pilot, risk assessment, and KPIs — not just one section.
  5. Use what you learned. Reference course concepts (responsible AI principles, data classification, shadow AI, etc.) naturally.
  6. Keep it readable. Clear headings, short paragraphs, and tables make your plan easier to evaluate (and easier to actually use).

This project is your chance to prove you can apply everything from this course to a real enterprise challenge. Good luck!
