Welcome

Welcome to AI for the Enterprise: From Zero to Secure Adoption. This course is designed to take you from having zero AI experience to being able to confidently evaluate, adopt, and govern AI tools in your organization.

You do not need a technical background. You do not need to write code. What you need is curiosity and a willingness to explore new tools with a critical, security-conscious mindset.

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

  • Explain what AI is (and isn't) in plain business language
  • Evaluate AI tools for real enterprise use cases
  • Identify security, privacy, and compliance risks before they become problems
  • Build a complete AI adoption plan for your organization
  • Earn a certificate of completion to demonstrate your new skills

Who This Course Is For

This course IS for you if:
- You are a business professional, manager, director, or executive
- You work in operations, finance, HR, marketing, IT, legal, or compliance
- You have heard about AI but haven't used it (or only casually)
- You need to make decisions about AI adoption at your organization
- You want a practical, no-hype understanding of what AI can actually do

This course is NOT for you if:
- You are looking for a deep technical course on building ML models from scratch
- You already have significant hands-on experience with enterprise AI deployment
- You want a purely theoretical or academic treatment of AI

Account Setup Guide

Throughout this course, you will use several free AI platforms for hands-on exercises. Set up accounts on these platforms before you begin Module 2.

1. ChatGPT (by OpenAI)

ChatGPT is a conversational AI tool for generating text, answering questions, summarizing documents, and brainstorming.

  1. Go to chatgpt.com
  2. Click Sign Up
  3. Enter your email address and create a strong password (mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols)
  4. Check your inbox for a verification email from OpenAI — click the link to activate
  5. Log in and try typing a message in the chat box at the bottom of the screen
  6. Free tier is sufficient for this course. You do not need ChatGPT Plus.

2. Claude (by Anthropic)

Claude is an AI assistant known for careful, nuanced responses and strong document analysis.

  1. Go to claude.ai
  2. Click Sign Up — you can use email, Google, or Apple login
  3. Create a password and verify your email
  4. Log in and start a conversation
  5. Free tier is sufficient for this course.

3. Google Gemini

Gemini is Google's AI assistant, integrated with the Google ecosystem.

  1. Go to gemini.google.com
  2. Sign in with your existing Google/Gmail account (no separate signup needed)
  3. Accept the terms of service
  4. Start chatting
  5. Free tier is sufficient for this course.

4. Hugging Face

Hugging Face is a community platform where thousands of AI models and datasets are shared openly. You will use it to explore what's available, not to build models.

  1. Go to huggingface.co
  2. Click Sign Up and enter your email
  3. Verify your email
  4. Complete your profile (name and bio are fine)
  5. Explore the Model Hub (browse by task: text generation, translation, sentiment analysis, etc.)
  6. Explore Spaces — these are live demos of AI applications you can try in your browser
  7. Free tier is sufficient for this course.

Security Reminder: Use unique, strong passwords for each platform. Consider using a password manager. Never enter your corporate credentials on these platforms.

AI Glossary for Business Professionals

Over 65 terms you will encounter in this course and in the workplace, explained in plain English.

A

Accuracy — How often an AI system gives the correct answer. A model that correctly identifies 95 out of 100 emails as spam has 95% accuracy.

Agent / AI Agent — An AI system that can take actions on its own (like booking a meeting or answering customer questions) without a human pressing buttons at each step.

Algorithm — A set of step-by-step instructions a computer follows to solve a problem. Think of it as a recipe.

Alignment — Making sure an AI system's behavior matches what humans actually want it to do. A misaligned AI might technically complete a task but in a harmful or unintended way.

API (Application Programming Interface) — A way for two software systems to talk to each other. When your company's app uses ChatGPT behind the scenes, it communicates through an API.

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) — A hypothetical future AI that could perform any intellectual task a human can. Does not exist today.

Artificial Intelligence (AI) — Computer systems designed to perform tasks that normally require human intelligence, such as understanding language, recognizing images, or making decisions.

Automation — Using technology to perform tasks without human intervention. AI-powered automation can handle more complex, judgment-based tasks than traditional automation.

B

Bias — When an AI system produces unfair or skewed results because of problems in its training data or design. Example: a hiring tool that favors one demographic over another.

Black Box — An AI system whose internal decision-making process is not visible or understandable to users. You see the input and output but not how it got there.

Bot / Chatbot — A software program that simulates conversation with humans, often used for customer service, FAQs, or internal helpdesks.

C

Classification — An AI task where the system assigns a category to something. Example: labeling emails as "spam" or "not spam."

Cloud Computing — Delivering computing services (storage, processing, AI tools) over the internet instead of running them on your own hardware.

Computer Vision — AI that can interpret and understand images and video. Used for quality inspection, security cameras, medical imaging, and more.

Compliance — Meeting legal, regulatory, and organizational requirements. AI compliance means ensuring your AI use follows laws like GDPR, HIPAA, or industry standards.

Conversational AI — AI systems designed to have human-like conversations, including chatbots and voice assistants.

D

Data — The raw information (text, numbers, images, records) that AI systems learn from and process.

Data Governance — The policies, processes, and standards for managing an organization's data — who can access it, how it's stored, and how quality is maintained.

Data Lake — A large storage system that holds raw data in its original format until it's needed for analysis.

Data Privacy — Protecting personal and sensitive information from unauthorized access or misuse.

Deep Learning — A type of machine learning that uses layered neural networks to find complex patterns. Powers image recognition, language translation, and speech-to-text.

Deepfake — AI-generated fake images, audio, or video that look and sound real. A growing security and misinformation concern.

E

Edge AI — Running AI directly on a device (phone, camera, sensor) instead of sending data to the cloud. Faster and more private for certain applications.

Embeddings — A way of converting text, images, or other data into numbers that capture meaning, so AI can compare and search them efficiently.

Ethical AI — Designing, building, and using AI systems in ways that are fair, transparent, and respectful of human rights.

Explainability — The ability to describe, in understandable terms, how an AI system reached a particular decision. Critical for regulated industries.

F

Few-Shot Learning — When an AI can learn a new task from just a few examples, rather than thousands.

Fine-Tuning — Taking a pre-trained AI model and training it a bit more on your specific data to make it work better for your use case.

Foundation Model — A large AI model (like GPT-4, Claude, or Gemini) trained on broad data that can be adapted for many different tasks.

G

Generative AI (GenAI) — AI that creates new content — text, images, code, music, video — based on patterns learned from training data. ChatGPT and DALL-E are examples.

Governance — The frameworks, policies, and oversight structures that ensure AI is used responsibly within an organization.

GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) — Specialized computer hardware that accelerates AI training and processing. Originally designed for graphics, now essential for AI workloads.

Guardrails — Rules, filters, or constraints put in place to prevent an AI system from producing harmful, inappropriate, or off-topic output.

H

Hallucination — When an AI confidently generates information that is incorrect or entirely made up. A critical reliability risk in enterprise settings.

Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) — A system design where a human reviews, approves, or corrects AI outputs before they are acted on. Essential for high-stakes decisions.

I

Inference — When a trained AI model processes new data and produces an output. The "using" phase, as opposed to the "training" phase.

Internet of Things (IoT) — A network of physical devices (sensors, machines, vehicles) connected to the internet that can collect and share data, often analyzed by AI.

K

Knowledge Base — A structured collection of information that an AI system can search and reference when answering questions.

L

Large Language Model (LLM) — A type of AI trained on massive amounts of text that can generate, summarize, translate, and reason about language. GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, and Llama are examples.

Latency — The time delay between sending a request to an AI system and receiving a response. Lower latency means faster answers.

M

Machine Learning (ML) — A branch of AI where systems learn patterns from data rather than being explicitly programmed. The engine behind most practical AI tools.

Model — The trained AI system itself — the thing that takes input and produces output. Built by processing large amounts of data.

Multimodal AI — AI that can work with multiple types of input (text, images, audio, video) at the same time.

N

Narrow AI — AI designed for one specific task (like spam filtering or product recommendations). All current commercial AI is narrow AI.

Natural Language Processing (NLP) — AI's ability to understand, interpret, and generate human language. Powers chatbots, translation, summarization, and search.

Neural Network — A computing system loosely inspired by the human brain, made up of layers of connected nodes that process information. The foundation of deep learning.

O

On-Premises (On-Prem) — Running software or AI on your own servers and hardware, as opposed to in the cloud. Offers more control but requires more maintenance.

Open Source — Software or AI models whose code is publicly available and can be freely used, modified, and distributed. Hugging Face hosts many open-source models.

Overfitting — When an AI model learns the training data too well (including its noise and quirks) and performs poorly on new, unseen data.

P

Parameter — A value inside an AI model that gets adjusted during training. Large models have billions of parameters. More parameters generally means more capability (but also more cost).

Personally Identifiable Information (PII) — Any data that could identify a specific person (name, email, SSN, address). Handling PII with AI requires strict data privacy controls.

Pilot Program — A small-scale, controlled test of an AI tool in a real business setting before a wider rollout.

Predictive Analytics — Using data, statistical algorithms, and AI to forecast future outcomes — like customer churn, equipment failures, or sales trends.

Prompt — The text instruction or question you give to an AI system. The quality of your prompt significantly affects the quality of the response.

Prompt Engineering — The skill of crafting effective prompts to get useful, accurate outputs from AI tools.

Prompt Injection — A security attack where a malicious user crafts a prompt that tricks an AI into ignoring its instructions or leaking sensitive information.

R

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) — A technique where an AI retrieves relevant information from a knowledge base before generating a response, improving accuracy and reducing hallucinations.

Regression — An AI task that predicts a number (like future revenue or temperature) rather than a category.

Responsible AI — A set of principles and practices for building and using AI in ways that are ethical, fair, transparent, accountable, and safe.

ROI (Return on Investment) — The financial value gained from an AI investment compared to its cost. A core metric for enterprise AI decisions.

S

Sentiment Analysis — AI that determines the emotional tone of text — positive, negative, or neutral. Used for customer feedback, social media monitoring, and brand tracking.

Shadow AI — When employees use unauthorized AI tools (personal ChatGPT accounts, unapproved plugins) without IT or security oversight. A major governance risk.

SLA (Service Level Agreement) — A contract defining the expected performance, uptime, and support levels for an AI service or vendor.

Structured Data — Data organized in rows and columns (like spreadsheets or databases). Easier for AI to process than unstructured data.

Supervised Learning — A type of machine learning where the AI learns from labeled examples. You show it inputs with correct answers, and it learns the pattern.

T

Tokenization — Breaking text into smaller pieces (tokens) that an AI model can process. A token might be a word, part of a word, or a punctuation mark.

Training — The process of teaching an AI model by feeding it data and adjusting its parameters until it performs well on a task.

Transfer Learning — Applying knowledge from a model trained on one task to a different (but related) task, saving time and data.

Transformer — The neural network architecture behind modern LLMs. Introduced in 2017, it revolutionized how AI processes language.

U

Unstructured Data — Data without a predefined format — emails, documents, images, audio, video. Makes up roughly 80% of enterprise data.

Unsupervised Learning — Machine learning where the AI finds patterns in data without labeled examples. Used for clustering, anomaly detection, and more.

V

Validation — Testing an AI model on data it hasn't seen during training to check how well it generalizes to new situations.

Vector Database — A database optimized for storing and searching embeddings. Powers semantic search and RAG systems.

W

Workflow Automation — Using AI and software to automate multi-step business processes, from invoice processing to employee onboarding.

Z

Zero-Shot Learning — When an AI can perform a task it was never specifically trained on, using its general knowledge. Example: asking an LLM to classify text into categories it's never seen before.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Do I need to know how to code?
A: No. This course is designed for non-technical professionals. All exercises use point-and-click interfaces.

Q: What if I've never used ChatGPT or any AI tool?
A: Perfect — that's exactly who this course is for. Module 2 walks you through your first conversation step by step.

Q: How long does the course take?
A: Most students complete it in 6–8 weeks at 3–5 hours per week. You can go faster or slower — it's self-paced.

Q: Is there a certificate?
A: Yes. Upon completing all modules, passing the final exam (70% or higher), and submitting the final project, you will receive a Certificate of Completion.

Q: Can I use my work data in the exercises?
A: We strongly recommend using fictional or anonymized data for all exercises. Never paste confidential business data into public AI tools.

Q: What if I get stuck?
A: Use the discussion board in Kajabi, email the course support team, or attend optional live Q&A sessions (schedule posted in Module 1).

Q: Will this course make me an AI developer?
A: No. It will make you an informed AI decision-maker — someone who can evaluate, adopt, and govern AI with confidence.

Q: Are the AI tool accounts free?
A: Yes. The free tiers of ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Hugging Face are sufficient for all course activities.
