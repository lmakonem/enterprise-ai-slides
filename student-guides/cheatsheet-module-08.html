What Is Prompt Engineering?

Prompt engineering is the skill of writing clear, structured instructions that get the best possible output from AI. Think of it as learning to brief a highly capable but literal-minded new employee.

The Universal Prompt Formula

ROLE  +  CONTEXT  +  TASK  +  FORMAT  +  CONSTRAINTS
Element What to Include Example
Role Who the AI should act as "You are a senior financial analyst…"
Context Background information "Our company is a mid-size SaaS firm with $50M ARR…"
Task What you want done "Analyze this quarterly report and identify…"
Format How to structure the output "Present as a bulleted executive summary…"
Constraints Rules and boundaries "Keep under 300 words. Use only data provided."

The CO-STAR Framework

A battle-tested framework for business prompts:

Letter Element Purpose
C Context Background info the AI needs
O Objective The specific goal
S Style Writing style (formal, casual, technical)
T Tone Emotional quality (confident, empathetic, neutral)
A Audience Who will read the output
R Response Desired format and length

CO-STAR Example

Context: Our Q3 revenue declined 12% due to customer churn in the SMB segment.

Objective: Draft talking points for the all-hands meeting explaining the decline and our recovery plan.

Style: Executive communication — clear, confident, data-driven.

Tone: Honest but optimistic. Acknowledge the challenge, emphasize the plan.

Audience: All employees (technical and non-technical).

Response: 5–7 bullet points, each 1–2 sentences. Include one key metric per point.

Prompt Patterns for Business

1. Chain of Thought

Ask the AI to reason step-by-step before answering.

"Walk me through your reasoning step by step before giving your recommendation for…"

2. Few-Shot Examples

Provide examples of desired input/output pairs.

"Here are two examples of how I want customer emails categorized:
Email: 'My order hasn't arrived' → Category: Shipping
Email: 'How do I reset my password?' → Category: Account Support
Now categorize this email: '...'"

3. Role + Constraint

Combine expertise with guardrails.

"You are an employment lawyer reviewing this termination letter. Flag any statements that could create legal liability. Do not rewrite the letter — only identify risks."

4. Structured Output

Request specific formats.

"Analyze this RFP and produce a table with columns: Requirement, Our Capability, Gap (Yes/No), Effort Estimate."

5. Iterative Refinement

Build on previous outputs.

"Now take the executive summary you just wrote and make it suitable for a board presentation — more concise, highlight only the three most critical findings."

Common Prompt Mistakes

Mistake Problem Fix
Too vague "Help me with marketing" Specify the exact task, audience, and format
No context AI doesn't know your situation Provide relevant background information
No format specified Output is unstructured Request bullets, tables, numbered lists, etc.
Too many tasks at once AI loses focus Break into sequential prompts
No constraints Output is too long or off-topic Set word limits, scope boundaries
Assuming AI knows your data It doesn't have your internal info Paste relevant data or use RAG

Temperature Guide for Business Prompts

Task Type Temperature Why
Data extraction, compliance checks 0.0 – 0.2 Need consistency and accuracy
Report writing, summarization 0.3 – 0.5 Balanced clarity
Marketing copy, brainstorming 0.6 – 0.8 More creative variation
Ideation, creative naming 0.8 – 1.0 Maximum diversity of ideas

Prompt Templates — Quick Reference

Email Drafting:

"Draft a professional email to [recipient/role] regarding [topic]. Tone: [formal/friendly/urgent]. Include: [key points]. Keep under [X] words."

Document Summarization:

"Summarize the following document in [X] bullet points for [audience]. Focus on [specific aspects]. Flag any items requiring action."

Decision Analysis:

"Given the following options: [list options]. Evaluate each against these criteria: [criteria]. Present as a comparison table with a recommendation."

Meeting Prep:

"Based on the following agenda and background materials, prepare: (1) Three key questions to ask, (2) Potential objections and responses, (3) A one-paragraph opening statement."
